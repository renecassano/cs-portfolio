The Academy Awards are an 89 year old hollywood annual ceremony that honors and awards achievements in film every year that is hosted by the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences. The very first Academy Awards ceremony was held on May 16, 1929, predating the mass production of penicillin by 14 years and postdating the birth of Betty White by 7. The Academy Awards ceremony, or the Oscars, is broadcasted live on television every year, graciously allowing those at home to gawk at the much more attractive-than-them and filthily rich celebrities on the red carpet, recognize some of the year’s greatest films and filmmakers, and laugh with simultaneous shock at the oh so rare but precious moments of severe blunders during the broadcast. Such mistakes include (but are not limited to) using a photo of the wrong person during the “In Memoriam” sequence, announcing the wrong winner for the Best Picture award (both just this past year!), horribly mispronouncing names, and the list goes on. A whole website could probably be committed to these kinds of mistakes, but unfortunately that is not the main point of this website.

As I said previously, the Oscars were established in 1929. This first ceremony was held at a private party being held at the Hollywood Roosevelt Hotel, where there was an audience of about 270 people, and 15 awards were handed out. Funnily enough, this first Oscars ceremony ran for a measly 15 minutes! That is nothing compared to the sometimes tiring average running time of 2-3 hours since the event was first televised in 1953. The crowd is also tiny compared to how many people usually attend the Oscars now. The ceremony is held at the Dolby Theater, which has a seating capacity of about 3,401, and just about every seat is taken at the ceremony! All this is funny to think about, the now cultural staple started as a very small and personal event in the late 20s. Back then, TV wasn’t even a thing, so you couldn’t see all the awkward interviews on the red carpet and the massive crowds and stunts at the ceremony.The Academy has a voting membership of around 6,000 people, all members of the Academy and a part of a specific discipline of film and its corresponding branch of the Academy. In 2012 the Los Angeles Times conducted a study that basically found that the Oscars were extremely white, with an astounding 94% of the Academy being Caucasian. As you might find in my writing on some of the categories though, there has been a lot of effort to try and fix the race issues, and there was much more diverse racial representation this year. Regardless of some of its issues, the ceremony has been a large part of American and film culture in general since its introduction, and was the basis for all of its just as huge later equivalent awards shows (Emmy Awards, Tony Awards, and Grammy Awards). BEST PICTURE

Obviously the biggest award of the ceremony, the Best Picture award is for the greatest films of the year as wholes. The rules regarding the number of nominees allowed each year has an interesting and kind of confusing history, but it goes like this: In the 30s and 40s up to 8-12 films could be nominated for Best Picture in a year. Afterwards, only 5 films could be nominated, at least until 2009! In 2009 the Academy announced that the number of nominees a year would go from 5 to 10. Just two years later though, this was changed so that between 5 and 10 films could be nominated. Just last year, only nine films were nominated for the big prize. This seems to be working for the Academy, although just last year there was some discontent with the 10th slot not being filled by a film they thought deserved it, such as Deadpool, or Sing Street. Unfortunately this is just how all the math that elects the final nominees that I don’t care to explain works out. 5-10 films can be nominated, and the number can easily fall anywhere in between.

Nine really cool films were nominated for best picture. It was the typical sort of dramas, history pieces and, smaller more personal projects that got the nomination. The films nominated were: Arrival, Fences, Hacksaw Ridge, Hell or High Water, Manchester By The Sea, Hidden Figures, La La Land, Lion, and the ultimate winner of the little naked gold dude statuette, Moonlight. There were a lot of easy jokes made by this year’s host, Jimmy Kimmel, at the ceremony about how most people probably didn’t see these pictures. Heck, here I am writing a whole website on the Oscars and the 2017 show and even I’ve only seen 7.5/9 of the movies. But from what I saw, I can tell ya these were all some pretty cool movies! They were all beautiful visually, in terms of story, acting ETC.

THE BIGGEST MESS UP IN OSCAR HISTORY!!!

It was the very end of the ceremony. Everyone in the room was probably just waiting for the final award, Best Picture, to be done with and go home or to some crazy Hollywood after party. LITTLE DID THEY KNOW WHAT WAS ABOUT TO GO DOWN. Faye Dunaway and Warren Beatty were announcing the winner. Warren Beatty was holding the envelope Warren Beatty looked down at the piece of paper a lot like how you might look down at an odd looking cat, but you don’t want to say anything because it’s your friend’s new cat and they’ve been excited about getting a cat for months, so you just try to play it nice and safe. Like, it looks like there might actually be something medically wrong with the cat but you don’t want to be rude. Anyway, Beatty then decided to just hand the paper off to Dunaway and let her read it to everyone. She then quickly announced the winner as La La Land. The director, stars, producers and everyone representing La La Land got up on stage to give all of their speeches. About 2 ½ producers got through their speeches, when someone people from backstage got on stage. Everyone representing La La Landwas sadly giving back their Oscars in the background after thinking they won something they probably dreamt about all their lives while a producer of La La Land, Jordan Horowitz, had to announce that there was a mess-up, and Moonlight had actually won Best Picture.

Originally, I had thought it was some cruel joke. La La Land had been the favorite to win the award all awards season, and Moonlight was usually listed as the close second. Because of this, in the first minute of confusion, I had assumed Jimmy Kimmel was working off of this and decided to have La La Land falsely announced before Moonlight. This made sense to me because of a combination of all the stuff Kimmel had been doing all night, the tasteless (but still admittedly funny) stuff some other people had done in previous years, and the minor shock at how something so simple could be messed up. My thoughts during the ceremony were “Damn, too far Kimmel, too far!!” However, i soon realized how ridiculous this was and that it was not the case, and Kimmel probably wouldn’t have the authority or support to do something as crazy like that.

What actually went down and how the mess-up happened was mostly revealed the next day. Beatty had been handed the wrong envelope backstage. The envelope he had was actually a back-up envelope with Emma Stone’s name as the winner for Best Actress for her role in La La Land, which was announced earlier in the ceremony (MORE ON THAT AFTER). After Beatty confusingly gave the envelope to Dunaway, she just saw “La La Land” and blurted that out. This whole situation was HUGE on social media. People on twitter were having a field day making memes out of it all, and media outlets unreasonably chose to focus on Beatty as the big culprit and buffoon. The Best Picture mess-up boosted an otherwise pretty good ceremony into being on the front of Oscars history and the top pick for every blogger’s “Top 10 Biggest Blunders in Oscar History!” list that following week.

BEST LEADING ACTOR

Just as with a lot of the Oscar categories in 2017 and in general, “Best Actor in a Leading Role” was a pretty competitive category. The nominees were: Andrew Garfield, for his role in Hacksaw Ridge, Denzel Washington for his role in Fences, Ryan Gosling for his role in La La Land, Viggo Mortensen for his role in Captain Fantastic, and the category’s winner: Casey Affleck for his role in Manchester by the Sea.

During awards season, Casey and Denzel were the favorites to win the award. For some time closer to the ceremony date though, some legal issues came back to haunt Casey Affleck. Around 2010, two women who had worked on Affleck’s 2010 mockumentary I’m Still Here, Amanda White and Magdalena Gorka filed lawsuits against him for sexual harassment over filming of the mockumentary. The two women recounted all sorts of instances of sexual harassment from Affleck and the film crew that went from saying things like “Isn’t it about time you got pregnant?” to Affleck drunkenly crawling into Gorka’s bed in the middle of the night while she slept. Both cases were eventually settled out of court, so there was no verdict or substantiation of the claims, and details of the settlements were not released to the public. Still, people on the internet framed it as a black-and-white case, portraying Affleck as a monster sneaking his way through Hollywood. The cases did greatly muddle Affleck’s reputation amongst some circles, and also seemingly, his chances of winning. Not only did this draw a lot of controversy and attention towards Affleck, it also earned some backlash against the Academy after eventually handing him his Oscar.

The run between Affleck and Washington was actually really interesting because of how differently they acted in both of their movies. Denzel was very loud and showy role with all of his character’s emotions fully on display, while Affleck was much quieter and subtle in his performance. Overall, 2017’s Best Leading Actor race was a two-man race between two very dissimilar performances, with three other extraordinary actors that will hopefully win their Oscars in the future.

BEST LEADING ACTRESS

2017’s Best Leading Actress race was one of the categories that seemed to have a clear winner from the start. The nominees were: Ruth Negga for her role in Loving, Natalie Portman for Jackie, a rare nomination for a performance in a French film: Isabelle Huppert for Elle, Meryl Streep for Florence Foster Jenkins, and the obvious winner from the start, Emma Stone for La La Land.

Stone was a favorite for a variety of reasons. La La Land received massive critical acclaim unlike that of all of the movie’s of the other actresses in the category (La La Land broke the record for most Golden Globes wins), which boosts the chances of the movie’s actors winning awards vastly, and she was winning all of the other awards, like a Golden Globe and SAG award, which are strongly correlated with who wins the Oscar. Also, even though one might consider her a sort of underdog for being in a foreign film (nobody really likes subtitles), Isabelle Huppert was considered Stone’s biggest competitor by some. This was due to Huppert also winning Best Leading Actress at the Golden Globes (there’s one drama category and a comedy/musical category for leading actors, Huppert won the award for drama and Stone won the musical category). So, even though La La Land lost the Best Picture award, Stone could luckily still go home with a statuette.

BEST DIRECTOR

Again, the winner seemed to be pretty easy to predict from the get-go. The nominees were: Denis Villeneuve for Arrival, Barry Jenkins for Moonlight, Kenneth Lonergan for Manchester by the Sea, Mel Gibson for Hacksaw Ridge, and the winner, Damien Chazelle for La La Land.

The only other film Damien Chazelle wrote and directed himself that got a wide-release was 2014’s Whiplash. It was one of the best movies of the year, and earned the director a lot of acclaim and the film earned 5 Academy Award nominations, 3 of which it won. Chazelle’s history with the Academy, combined with the impressive beauty and mastery of La La Land, and the fact that the movie centered around one of Hollywood’s favorite subjects: Hollywood made him the favorite to win. Damien Chazelle also became the youngest person to ever win “Best Director”, he was only 32 when he won. All of the other directors also made amazing and thought-provoking films. Mel Gibson produced very striking images of war that helped the viewer feel as if they were on the battlefield, Lonergan very much utilized the setting of Manchester-by-the-Sea as its own identifiable character, and got amazing performances out of the actors. Jenkins used cinematic methods and created images to create a very distinct and beautiful style for his movie, and Villeneuve again proved his brilliance by making one of the most intriguing and thought-provoking films of the year.

BEST SUPPORTING ACTOR

Another easy lock for the award, Best Supporting Actor didn’t spark too much debate on who would win. Nominees in the category this year were Jeff Bridges, for his role in Hell or High Water, Lucas Hedges for his role in Manchester by the Sea, Dev Patel for his role in Lion, Michael Shannon for his role in Nocturnal Animals and the winner, Mahershala Ali for his role in Moonlight.

While all of the actors delivered fine and memorable performances, Ali seemed to stick with people more than everyone else. Ali portrayed a sort of “drug dealer with a heart of gold” in the first third of Moonlight - before the movie moved onto the other 2 pivotal points of the main character’s life being depicted in the film - Ali acted as a sort of father figure to Little during his screen time, and made viewers wonder where the heck he went when he didn’t appear in the rest of the movie with almost no explanation. I haven’t seen Lion still, which is the movie Dev Patel was in, but from what I’ve heard, it’s arguable whether Patel was a leading or supporting actor in the film. With ensemble movies, a lot of actors that had bigger parts in movies can sneak into the supporting actor category if the competition in the category seems less threatening than whoever is up for the leading actor category. This was definitely the case with Viola Davis this year, she was in Fences, which I actually did see but more on that in the supporting actress category!

Lucas Hedges and Jeff Bridges also delivered strong performances in their films. Both roles had a specific moment/scene where they really got to show off their acting chops that could probably be credited with earning them the Oscar nods. Michael Shannon was extremely compelling as detective Bobby Andes, and what drew almost as much attention as his performance was the fact that he got the nomination over his co-star, Aaron Taylor-Johnson,who actually won the Golden Globe for best supporting actor in a drama the month prior to the Oscars. So there was some confusion over why Shannon then got the nomination for the Oscar.

While there is some overlap in voters between the Golden Globes and the Oscars, they do consist of different members, so I guess Oscar people just liked Shannon more, after all he had been nominated before in 2009. Overall, just as with every category almost every year, the Best Supporting Actor category was full of amazing talent.

BEST SUPPORTING ACTRESS

Best Supporting Actress was probably the most competitive category of the year, with two very even frontrunners and one more actress very close behind in terms of amounts of praise and talk the performances generated. The two frontrunners were Viola Davis for Fences and Michelle Williams for Manchester by the Sea. Naomie Harris was also in great contention for her role in Moonlight, and Nicole Kidman and Octavia Spencer also earned nominations for their roles in Lion and Hidden Figures respectively (but I still haven’t seen Lion and only half of Hidden Figures).

As I said, Davis did go on to win the award, but there was some talk over whether she belonged in this category in the first place. It’s very easy to argue that she was more of a co-lead rather than a supporting actress alongside Denzel. Davis’ agent or whoever probably made a push for her to be put in the supporting actress category because the competition was thought to not be as bad as who was up for best leading actress. The fact that Viola had way more screen time in her film than everyone else in this category gave her a pretty big advantage, but nonetheless, you can’t argue that she delivered a great performance, with amazing believability, authentic real cry-snot and everything. Michelle Williams was actually my own favorite to win, she managed to do a lot and leave a huge impression in the little time she had on screen, and she stole every scene she was in. Naomie Harris also did phenomenally in Moonlight, especially once you find out that she only had 3 days to do all of her scenes in the middle of press touring for Spectre. I can’t speak much on the other two nominees, as I barely saw the movies they were in, but I’m sure they were great too.
